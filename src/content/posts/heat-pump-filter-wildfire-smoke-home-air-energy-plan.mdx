A heat pump can support comfort during wildfire-smoke days, but it is not automatically a clean-air plan. Filter condition, window habits, portable air cleaner placement, fan settings, room selection, and vulnerable residents all matter. This guide was checked on 2026-06-22 against EPA, AirNow, DOE, CDC, and ENERGY STAR resources. It is not HVAC design, medical, or electrical advice; follow equipment manuals, local smoke alerts, utility guidance, and qualified professional recommendations when they are more specific.

## Quick decision table

| Situation | Safer choice | Mistake to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Smoke forecast is rising | Prepare one clean-air room and inspect filters | Waiting until the room already smells smoky |
| Filter is dirty or unknown | Follow manual-compatible replacement guidance | Forcing a filter the system cannot handle |
| Heat and smoke overlap | Prioritize vulnerable people and safe cooling | Opening windows for comfort when outdoor smoke is worse |
| Portable cleaner is used | Place it safely with clear airflow and cords | Blocking airflow or overloading outlets |

## 3. Balance comfort, recirculation, and energy load
When two warnings overlap, choose the conservative branch. Heat plus smoke, fatigue plus chest symptoms, outdoor serving
plus raw-food transport, or poor visibility plus a long unfamiliar route should not be treated as isolated
inconveniences. Stack the warnings in plain language, then reduce intensity, delay the trip, keep food cold, prepare a
clean-air room, or ask for qualified help. This is also the point where the article stays policy-safe: it avoids miracle
